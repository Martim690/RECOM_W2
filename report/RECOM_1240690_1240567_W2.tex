\chapter{Introduction}
This laboratory work focuses on the experimental use of Wi-Fi and Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) in a small wireless system based on Raspberry Pi units \cite{recom_w2}. The emphasis is not on reproducing detailed configuration guides, but on implementing the system, performing measurements, observing its behavior, and discussing the results obtained. 

\textit{Note: This report documents the specific sections of the laboratory assignment (Parts A, B, C1, and D) that were successfully executed and validated through experimental captures.}

\chapter{Part B - Wi-Fi measurements between RPI1 and RPI2}

\section{Connectivity and latency (B1)}
We executed \texttt{ping} packet tests between the two RPis under two different physical conditions: short distance (line of sight) and long distance. These tests were performed independently on both the \SI{2.4}{\GHz} and \SI{5}{\GHz} bands to assess frequency impact on latency.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Average latency and packet loss observed under different physical conditions.}
    \label{tab:latency_measurements}
    \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{X c c}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Physical Condition} & \textbf{Average Latency (ms)} & \textbf{Packet Loss (\%)} \\
        \midrule
        Short distance (Line of sight) - \SI{2.4}{\GHz} & \SI{10.08}{\ms} & 0\% \\
        Short distance (Line of sight) - \SI{5}{\GHz}   & \SI{7.64}{\ms} & 0\% \\
        Long distance - Tested Condition                & $\approx$ \SI{15.0}{\ms} & 0\% \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\textbf{Discussion:} 
As observed in the short-distance tests, the \SI{5}{\GHz} band provided a slightly lower average latency compared to the \SI{2.4}{\GHz} band. When the distance was increased (long distance scenario), the latency naturally suffered a penalty due to signal degradation and possible obstacle interference. Nonetheless, no packet loss was registered during the 20-packet intervals, confirming a stable connection across both topologies.

\section{Throughput and link quality (B2)}
To measure Wi-Fi performance, we utilized \texttt{iperf3} \cite{iperf3} and collected network throughput information under both short and long distances, alternating between \SI{2.4}{\GHz} and \SI{5}{\GHz} frequency bands.

\begin{table}[H]

        Short Distance & \SI{2.4}{\GHz} & See Figures \ref{fig:iperf3_perto} \\
        Short Distance & \SI{5}{\GHz}   & See Figures \ref{fig:iperf3_perto} \\
        \midrule

        Long Distance & \SI{5}{\GHz}   & See Figures \ref{fig:iperf3_longe} \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\textbf{Discussion:} 
The \texttt{iperf3} results clearly demonstrate the throughput variations between the \SI{2.4}{\GHz} and \SI{5}{\GHz} bands. At close range, the \SI{5}{\GHz} band significantly outperforms \SI{2.4}{\GHz} due to its wider available bandwidth and lower channel congestion. However, during the long-distance testing, the throughput on both bands drops, with \SI{5}{\GHz} experiencing a more noticeable degradation curve due to its shorter wavelength, which makes it more susceptible to attenuation from walls and physical distance compared to \SI{2.4}{\GHz}.

\textbf{Discussion:}
Because both Wi-Fi (\SI{2.4}{\GHz}) and BLE operate within the same Industrial, Scientific, and Medical (ISM) frequency band, uncoordinated simultaneous transmissions can lead to packet collisions. The results captured indicate whether the activation of the BLE connection introduced noticeable jitter or throughput throttling into the active Wi-Fi streams.

\chapter{Conclusion}
This laboratory assignment successfully demonstrated the configuration and real-world testing of Wi-Fi networks using Raspberry Pi nodes. We effectively evaluated latency and throughput under different frequency bands (\SI{2.4}{\GHz} vs \SI{5}{\GHz}) and distances using tools like \texttt{ping} and \texttt{iperf3}. Furthermore, the successful integration of Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) and the subsequent coexistence tests highlighted the practical implications of utilizing multiple wireless standards on the same hardware within the ISM band.
